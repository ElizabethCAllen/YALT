  1. How to pick out Fruit

    2. With apricots and other stone fruits (peaches, plums,etc), the skin should be unbroken and evenly colored with no browned or mushy spots. Plums should be smooth-skinned. Give the fruit a very gentle squeeze. If it’s hard, the fruit will be tart and firm. If there is a little give, it is probably just ripened and sweet but firm. If the fruit is softer, it will be soft inside and very sweet.

    Pears should be hard or very firm with no bruised spots. At home, they ripen very quickly, so don’t wait too long to use them.

    Tomatoes should be brightly colored, slightly soft when squeezed, with shiny skins and no mushy spots.

    Apples should be quite firm and evenly colored, with no gouges,cuts, holes, or brown or mushy spots.

    Melons should be hard, heavy for their size, and for cantaloupes and honeydews, the navel spot should smell like a good ripe melon tastes. Watermelons should be shiny, clean, and golden on the bottom side. Thunk the melon with your finger.You want a melon with a medium pitch, not too deep (overripe) or too high (green).

    Citrus fruit should be firm, with shiny, tight skins. When you squeeze it, it should seem full of juice. Fruit with thick skins is often old and dry.

    Strawberries should be medium-sized and red, not large or yellow in places. Look at the bottom of the container to check for mold.

    Cherries, raspberries, and blueberries from the supermarket are all pretty uniform in taste and quality. Home grown or freshly picked is always better, if you can get it.

    Bananas are mostly a matter of personal taste. Green or freckled, it just depends on your preference.

    Avocados are tricky. Green shiny ones might not be as perfect as they seem. A lot depends on when you are going to use the fruit and how. For guacamole you will make today, you want avocados that are soft but not mushy. If you buy hard ones, it won’t work. It doesn’t matter whether the outside is attractive, it only matters that, when you squeeze them ever so gently, the have a bit of give. If the little black button stem comes off easily, it’s ripe. Avocados will ripen in your non refrigerated fruit basket, but the rate of ripening is not 100 percent predictable. If you need them to be used in a fresh salad in three days, buy the ones that have only a little softness to them. If you need them in a week, go ahead and buy harder ones.

    Grapes are a crap-shoot. What looks delectable can be sour and awful. It’s best to rely on a good local produce department to offer better grapes rather than just rolling the dice and hoping.

    Pineapples do not ripen after picking. A ripe pineapple’s stem end will smell sweet. Choose a pineapple that has a little give when you squeeze it, and one of the topmost leaves should come off with ease when you pull it gentle.

  2. How to pick out vegetables

    3. Potatoes and other root vegetables, such as beets and carrots, should be hard, with no scars or gouge marks in the skin where processing machinery cut into the produce. There are several different colors of potatoes, carrots, beets, etc. The big brown potatoes called russets are for baking, and little potatoes are called new potatoes and are for mashed potatoes and other uses. Smaller potatoes should have smooth skins.

    Celery should be light in color and very firm, with crisp, fresh leaves.

    Asparagus should be small and very firm. It should have longer flexible spear tops and less inedible hard lower stems

    Onions come in different types. Spanish onions are reddish-purple and mild tasting. Sweet onions usually are more flat in shape and come in white and yellow varieties. The most commonly used kinds are globe yellow or white onions. Onions should be dry and very firm and heavy for their size. Avoid buying ones with black areas anywhere on the skin, because this is a mold that can make short work of your onion once it gets home. The flatter onions are usually sweeter and more aromatic. Green onions are baby onions with a unique flavor. The stalks should be firm and not floppy, and the bulbs at the bottom should be shiny, and feel tightly packed

    Corn on the cob should be just as fresh as possible. Look for full, heavy ears that have dry, brown silk on the ends. The kernels should be small, not large and coarse.

    Cucumbers are best when small and fresh. Fresh cucumbers are firm like an apple and shiny. Smaller ones are sweeter and have fewer seeds.

    Zucchini is a kind of squash. It’s green, with slightly fuzzy, mottled skin. Young, firm squash make the best eating. Good sliced in salads or sauteed with onions and garlic.

    Summer squash or crookneck squash is a curved yellow squash with a thin, bright yellow skin. Young, very firm squash make the best eating.

    Broccoli is a cluster of flowers. Old broccoli is floppy, bitter, and starting to bloom with yellow flowers. Good broccoli has tightly closed, dense heads and a deep blue-green color.

    Cabbage should be brightly colored and quite firm and heavy. It is easily mistaken for lettuce, and visa versa, so make sure it's cabbage that you are buying.

    Lettuce comes in many kinds and colors. More nutritious types are brightly colored. Arugula and and some others can be slightly bitter, which works best when combined with other flavors. Lettuce should look clean and feel crispy, and have little to no discoloration from the natural colors. Iceberg lettuce is nearly nutrition-free. Look for firm heads that don’t have a browned stem. Butter lettuce is a soft, sweet variety that comes in rounded heads. Red and green leaf lettuces are longer leaved salad standards. Romaine is a leafy lettuce used in Caesar salad or to add crunch to a mixed greens bowl.

    Fresh spinach should be tender, but not floppy, and bright green, not dulled to a waxy, tired-looking color.

    Fresh green beans are often offered when they have passed their prime. Look for tender, moist-looking beans with a bright color. Avoid scarred, dulled beans.

    Peppers should be very firm, not soft or wrinkled, and brightly colored. Green sweet Bell peppers ripen into yellow, orange, and red peppers. The only difference is the degree of ripeness.

  3. How to pick out non-plant produce

    4. Mushrooms should be dry, very firm, and evenly colored. Mushroom caps should be tightly closed, with no gills underneath exposed.Never slimy or soft and floppy.

  4. How to choose cuts of meat

    5. You can find a universe of wonderful recipes on the internet or in magazines or bookstores. Knowing how to choose the best ingredients is not usually included but is vital to success. The most common meats in the American diet are beef, chicken, pork, lamb, and seafood. When buying meat, remember that meat can always be contaminated.

    Beef, lamb, or tuna can be eaten rare, but that does not apply to other meats, as a rule. Cook other processed meat well to prevent food-borne illness. You will need a meat thermometer.

    Processed meat is what is usually found at the grocery store. It arrives at the meat department sealed in plastic and is cut up and wrapped in the store. Sometimes a butcher shop or country store will have meat that is not processed in a factory. Sometimes a butcher shop has the same kind of meat found at the grocery store, however. You will just have to ask. In addition, the category of processed meat includes “lunch meats,” and meat found in frozen dinners, snacks, and ready-to-eat entrees. These industrial products may contain dyes, trimmings, undesirable parts of the animal, preservatives, MSG, salt water, soy, or pink slime. (See below.)

    At the deli counter of your grocery store or in the meat department, look for meat that is shaped like a roast or natural cut of meat, not like a tube or oval. Look for natural muscle grain in the meat, and not a uniform texture that includes air bubbles. A lot of lunch meat is basically a cooked slop made of meaty bits, chemicals, salt, and water, with air whipped in to add volume. It’s more like a sponge than like meat. This piece will not even contemplate sausage.

    Mass produced hamburger and beef-based processed meats may contain “pink slime,” which may contain ammonia. Pink slime is not allowed for human consumption in the EU and Canada, and for good reason.

  5. How to choose cuts of Beef

    6. Tough cuts of meat are best prepared by stewing, pressure cooking, or other moist cooking method. Finer cuts can be roasted or broiled.

    A good cut of beef should be soft and pliable. Press on it with your fingertips to see if it has some give, like foam rubber or a pillow. If it’s harder, that means it has more connective tissue in it, which is what makes meat tough.

    Grain-fed beef is often thought superior to grass-fed beef. It has more fat marbled throughout and is less chewy, but grass-fed beef is leaner and more natural than grain-fed. Grass- fed cows walk around the pasture all day grazing, and although they develop stronger muscles, that means tougher meat.

    Aging: “dry-aged” beef is kept in airy but nearly frozen storage for about a month and is drier, denser, and more flavorful. Wet-aged meat is vacuum- sealed to prevent water loss. Fresh beef is less tender than aged.

    The grades of beef established by the US Department of Agriculture are, in order of amounts of marbling, are prime, choice, and select. Prime beef is usually sold to commercial businesses like restaurants.

    Hamburger—buy hamburger from a store that grinds their own. Hamburger needs to have some fat, but not more than about 20 percent.

    Steaks—many cuts of meat are named “steak.”

    Tenderloin or filet mignon is a small but tender and delicious part of the beef.

    T-bone and a porterhouse steaks are part “strip” steak and part “tenderloin” or filet mignon. A porterhouse steak is larger and has more tenderloin on it.

    Rib-eye steaks are well marbled and often have a rind of fat around them. Rib- eyes may be the most delectable cut. Prime rib is cooked over a bed of rock salt.

    Strip steak or New York strip steak is a marbled and flavorful cut that is usually not as soft as those described above. Be especially careful to press down to judge the amount of connective tissue in the meat.

    Top sirloin and sirloin steak are similar but top sirloin is of superior quality. Top sirloin is one of the better cuts of beef, along with the above steak types. Sirloin is a step down from top sirloin.

  6. How to pick out cuts of pork

    7. All pork needs to be cooked to 160 degrees Fahrenheit because a trichinosis worm infestation can be passed to humans from undercooked pork.

    Bacon should be cut thick and be dry and meaty, not slimey and mostly fat. Some grocery store deli departments have good bacon. Packaged bacon can be too wet and cut so thin that it’s hard to pull one strip off without tearing it.

    Use salt pork in recipes calling for a ham hock or bacon fat. It’s almost but not quite bacon.

    Save bacon fat. Pork fat is nature’s key to flavor, and bacon drippings have many uses. Add them to the oil you are using to sautee or fry fish, meat, or eggs. Add it to meatloaf, stews, cornbread, or gravy. Baste your turkey with it, brush it on a roast, or use it to grease a bread pan.

    Pork loin chops, bone in or bone out, can be pretty savory and tender. I look for white meat, not red meat, on the chop. The dark meat tastes different, gamier than the white meat. Chops can be baked or pan fried or cooked in a pressure cooker.

    Prociutto is a sweet and salty cured Italian-style ham that is widely available.

    A good ham is hard to come by these days. The best ham can be very expensive and is dry, salty, and savory. A Honeybaked Ham is usually the best available where I live, but online you can discover some really wonderful country hams. The kind that is best varies over time, so look for current online information from gourment cooking magazine sites. Unfortunately, most grocery store hams are so infused with salt water that they cannot even call themselves hams but must use the term “ham and water product.” To use them, you have to cook the water out first, which leaves them too salty.

    Smoked pork chops, if available, are drier and taste fine for use in fried rice, casseroles, and omlettes.

    Pork sausage is best made from grocery store plain ground pork, using your own seasonings.

    Ground pork is also good for tacos, fillings, meatloaf, and so on.

  7. How to pick out seafood

    8. Use a most reputable seafood market. It’s a good idea to ask whether the fish arrived frozen or fresh, where it originated, whether it’s wild or farmed, and what kind it is if that is not clear. Farmed fish is greener but wild fish taste better and a lot of fish is mislabeled intentionally. Fresh is better than frozen but not always available.

    Country of origin may be of concern. Imported seafood, especially shrimp, tilapia, and eel, can be a hazard to health. Viet Nam and the Philippines have had contamination issues in the past, as has China. Hepatitis A, toxins, and heavy metals have been problems, historically.

    When buying imported seafood, ask yourself whether you trust that country’s health and food safety standards, because hardly any imported seafood is inspected, let alone tested, by the US FDA. Most canned seafood is imported, and lots of frozen and fresh seafood is also from foreign countries.

    When buying seafood you may want to consult seafoodwatch.org to make sure you choose varieties that are not being over fished.

    The best rules of thumb about seafood is that it should smell like clean seawater, not like anything else, and, again, that you should use the most reputable seafood market. Check YELP. Any fish that smells “fishy” or like the beach at low tide is too old to buy. When buying a whole fish, the eye should be clear, not cloudy and the skin should be shiny and bright, not dried-out looking. Shellfish are especially tricky to evaluate for freshness. Bad shellfish can smell like garbage, but you can’t tell that if it’s frozen. This is where your market must be trustworthy.Frozen seafood from Trader Joe’s is pretty reliable for freshness.

  8. Food safety

    9. Food poisoning can be an ordeal and can be fatal in some cases. Thus, it is very important to use good sanitary practices in the kitchen.

    It’s best to refrigerate all perishable foods as soon as you can manage it. That said, perishable foods should not be left out more than one or two hours before being put into the fridge. Foods containing rare meat or egg should be refrigerated immediately. When in doubt, throw it out.

    The most dangerous foods to handle are seafoods, raw chicken, and raw pork.

    Have a separate plastic cutting board you use only for raw chicken and raw pork and bleach it after use.

    Be mindful of cross-contamination. If you just cut up some raw chicken, wash the counter with a bleach cleaner and paper towels, use a different cutting board, and use a clean knife to prep your salad ingredients or anything else. And wash your hands in between.

    Always wash your hands when you return home from being in public.

    Cleaning the tools

    Wash all utensils and plates, cutlery, etc., in a way that kills germs. Bleach is the best kitchen sanitizer.

    Use a dishwasher with a heat-dry function, which sterilizes most items, or add a half-cup of bleach at the rinse cycle.

    To wash items by hand, have ready: dish soap, a clean wash rag, an abrasive scrubbing pad, a bottle brush, a clean towel, hot, soapy water, a bleach rinse water, a dish drainer or a couple of clean kitchen towels, and hot running water.

    Hand washed items should be rinsed in dilute bleach water – roughly a gallon of water to a third of a cup of bleach—then in clean hot water. Anything wooden rinsed in bleach water should not be left to soak.

    Remember that nothing tells your guests you are a slob like handing them a greasy glass to drink from or a crusty fork to eat with.

    How to wash the dishes:

    Scrape all excess food from items. Soak dirty dishes in a sink or plastic tub of hot tap water with dish soap in it.

    Hand wash each object with a dishrag dipped in hot, soapy water. Carefully wash every surface, including the insides, outsides, and undersides of all items. Take care to clean handles and the undersides of rims. Make sure to double-check each object.

    Immerse all items in the hot bleach-water for about five minutes.

    Rinse each item in clean, hot water, then place in the dish drainer or on the clean towels to air-dry. Instead, you could dry everything with a clean towel and then put it away.

    To clean burnt-on food that a scrubbing pad will not remove, dab on a paste made of baking soda and water and let stand for 30 minutes. Then wipe and rinse, and try scrubbing again if some remains.

    Kitchen cleanliness

    Wash your hands often. Dry them on clean towels changed daily or paper towels.

    The sink should be cleaned every couple of days. Use a scouring powder or whatever is recommended by the sink manufacturer. Be sure to catch every corner and edge, the faucet, handles, the back splash, and behind the faucet.

    Wash sink drain strainers and plugs, bottle brushes, sponges, scrubbing pads, etc., in the dishwasher, then soak in bleach-water for five minutes.

    Throw out sponges that are ratty or slimy. Throw out worn out brushes and scrubbers sooner rather than later. New ones are much less germy and work better.

    It’s very important to keep counters clean and sanitary. Scrub well daily and rinse in bleach water. You can let it stand for a few minutes if the surface allows. Smooth stone surfaces can be cleaned with a razor scraper if need be.

    Wipe down the hood vent and other stove surfaces at least once a week. These tend to accumulate grease and particles.

    Cupboard surfaces should be wiped clean once a week or as needed. The tops of drawers and doors can need special attention.

    Close all drawers and cabinets while handling food.

    Place a knife on the counter far from the edge and certainly not protruding from the edge, where it could easily be knocked off or otherwise become a danger.

    Use knives, peelers, ice picks, and other sharp tools in a motion away from your body, not toward it, to avoid injury.

    Pan handles should never protrude from the edge of the stove.

    Never drop anything into boiling water or hot fat in such a way that it could make a splash. Lower things gently with tongs or a spoon.

    Sweep the kitchen floor daily and mop as needed or once a week.

    A refrigerator should be cleaned outside once a week and inside about once a month. Baking soda paste can remove tough food stains.

  9. Do's and don'ts of cooking

    10. Do's of cooking

    Do wash your hands for 20 seconds before and after any food.

    Do use expiration dates as general guidelines.

    Do use a meat thermometer

    Do clean all your surfaces and sinks where any raw meats or juices may have touched.

    Do wash produce.

    Don'ts of cooking

    Don’t allow leftovers to cool to room temperature before refrigerating them.

    Don’t wash meat and poultry: Rinsing raw meat and poultry, can actually increase your chances of spreading nasty bacteria.

    Don’t use the same surface and sinks where any raw meats or juices may have touched.

    Don’t soak meat in salt water in an effort to remove bacteria

  14. Roasting meat

    15. Roasts are cuts of beef before they are sliced into individual steaks. Always cook roasts with the fat side up. If they have no fat, you can lay strips of bacon on top or baste with olive oil and butter as they cook.

    Tenderloin roast is the filet, a small but excellent cut of beef.

    Rib roast or rib-eye roast is an excellent and toothsome larger roast for special occasions.

    Top sirloin and sirloin roasts are best cooked slowly or with a moist cooking method.

    Tri-tip is a West Coast name for a particular part of the sirloin that is sort of triangular in shape. Tri-tip is of inconsistent quality. Do the press test to find a palatable steak. Some can be really tough and gristly.

    Sirloin roasts, eye of round roasts, bottom round roasts, and any other kind of roast cuts are best prepared with moist methods, such as stew, soup, pepper steak, a casserole, or pot roast—in any case in a covered container with liquid in it.

    Cross-rib roasts are not worth the effort it might take to make them edible.

    Pork loin roast can also be very good when slow cooked and well seasoned. Slice it thin and offer a gravy or sauce because the meat can be a little dry.

    Pork shoulder roasts are best used in chile verde or other wet-method cookery.

    Spareribs are fatty and flavorful. They should be cooked until the fat is mostly melted away and the meat is falling off the bone. Marinate first if the recipe calls for that.

    Country-style ribs should be meaty. They can be tricky. Find a tried and true recipe for these.

  3. Prepping produce and cutting

    4. Wash all produce before cutting.

    Potatoes—peel and remove the eyes. Depending on your use and recipe, small potatoes may have part of the skin left on, or even all of it.

    Sweet peppers—cut in half and remove stem, inner pith, and seeds, then slice or chop.

    Asparagus—using your hands, gently break lower, tough part of the stem off completely. It will naturally snap where it gets inedible.

    Onion—peel, then cut up. If it is making your eyes tear up, hold it under running water or wash it off a few times as you chop it. Soak in cold water overnight to take away the strong smell.

    Garlic—take cloves off the bulb and peel them by crushing them with the side of a cleaver or the pressing them between a cutting board and the bottom of a sturdy plastic jar. Then just pick off the husks.

    Lettuce and other fresh greens—be careful to double rinse and double check every leaf of fresh greens for damage or insects, because ladybugs are not good in a salad. Tear up or cut up for your dish.

    Green Beans—cut off each end of the bean pod and snap in half by hand.

    Carrots—peel, then cut off the top and bottom ½ inch.

    Apples—peel, then core and cut into eighths. There is a special tool to make coring easier.

    Oranges—score the outside of the skin with a table knife or other blunt instrument. Cut it all the way around two or three times. Then make a small hole at the opposite side from the stem and peel off the skin with your fingers.

    Lemons Or Limes—a lemon squeezing press is a popular kitchen tool, as is an old fashioned juicing dish.

    Mushrooms—wash well, cut off the bottoms of the stems, slice into thick pieces, and saute until tender on low heat in butter. Add a little olive oil to raise the temperature at which butter starts to burn. A bit of onion and garlic add the perfect touch. Don’t overcook—tender but not rubbery.

    Zucchini, Summer Squash—peel, slice into 1/3 inch coins, then treat as for mushrooms.

    Tomatoes—slice or chop, according to recipe directions. To “deslime” tomatoes, cut them in half, then squeeze out the seeds and juice.

    Cabbage—remove the outer layer or two of leaves and trim off the stem bottom.

    Celery—wash especially well. To make celery tender, break the stalks, then pull off the “strings” that go down the stems. The leaves and inside stalks are especially useful in cooking

    Peaches or other stone fruit—to skin stone fruit, dip briefly in boiling water, then immerse in cold water. The skin should slip right off with the slightest effort.

    Strawberries—use a spoon, a straw or small corer to hull strawberries. A straw also works if the berries are not large.

    Broccoli—cut off the bottoms of the stems and cut into uniform-sized pieces. It doesn’t matter how big or small, but if they are the same size, they will cook to the same degree. That way there are no mushy bits and under-cooked bits in the same dish.

    Chard—cut off the ends of the stems and slice into largish pieces.

    Green onions—cut off the tops of the greens and the dirty part of the root end. If they are oldish, you may need to remove the top layer of the skins.

    Radishes—cut off the green tops and the roots.

    Cucumber—peel, cut off both ends, then slice or chop as needed.

  1. How to make rice

    2. There are many different types of rice, each with unique flavor, fragrance, and consistency qualities and rice-to-water ratios. Different cultures use different rice, such India use basmati rice and jasmine rice. While Japan uses short grain rice which clumps together and is perfect for eating with chopsticks and for pressing together for sushi or onigiri. Some rices are better for certain dishes than others, like basmati and long grain rice are good for pilafs, while fragrant jasmine rice and short grain rice are good for side dishes or for eating with curry. In regards to that water ratios, that information should be on the package somewhere, so make sure you find it before cooking the rice or storing it in a jar or other container.

    To cook in a pan, make sure you stir it every so often so it cooks evenly and doesn’t stick to the bottom of the pan or worst burn.

  4. How to make biscuits

    Ingredients

    • 2 Cups of all purpose flour
    • 1/2 teaspoon salt
    • 1-tablespoon of baking powder
    • 3/4 cup milk or whole milk
    • 1/2 cup shortening

    Instructions

    1. Preheat oven to 450 degrees F (230 degrees C).
    2. In a large mixing bowl sift together flour, baking powder and salt. Cut in shortening with a fork or pastry blender until mixture resembles coarse crumbs.
    3. Pour milk into flour mixture while stirring with a fork. Mix in milk until dough is soft, moist and pulls away from the side of the bowl.
    4. Turn dough out onto a lightly floured surface and toss with flour until no longer sticky. Roll the dough out into a 1/2 inch thick sheet and cut with a floured biscuit or cookie cutter. Press together unused dough and repeat rolling and cutting procedure.
    5. Place biscuits on ungreased baking sheets and bake in preheated oven until golden brown, about 10 minutes.
  5. How to make yeast rolls

    Ingredients

    • 2 tablespoons shortening
    • 3 tablespoons white sugar
    • 1 cup hot water
    • 1 (.25 ounce) package active dry yeast
    • 1 egg, beaten
    • 1 teaspoon salt
    • 2 1/4 cups all-purpose flour

    Instructions

    1. In a large bowl, mix the shortening, sugar, and hot water. Allow to cool until lukewarm, and mix in the yeast until dissolved. Mix in the egg, salt, and flour. Allow the dough to rise until doubled in size
    2. Grease 8 muffin cups. Divide the dough into the prepared muffin cups, and allow to rise again until doubled in size.
    3. Preheat oven to 425 degrees F (220 degrees C).
    4. Bake for 10 minutes in the preheated oven, or until a knife inserted in the center of a muffin comes out clean.
